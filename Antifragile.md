## Definition
Systems that not only withstand stress and volatility but actually improve and grow stronger from it. The opposite of fragile isn't robust—it's antifragile.

## Key Insight
Fragile things break under stress. Robust things resist stress. Antifragile things benefit from stress (up to a point). The best systems aren't just protected from chaos—they're designed to gain from it.

## How to Apply
Design systems with:
- Small, frequent stressors (rather than rare catastrophic ones)
- Redundancy and optionality
- Skin in the game (direct feedback)
- Limited downside, unlimited upside

Ask: "How could this benefit from volatility and disorder?"

## Real-World Example
Your immune system is antifragile. Exposure to germs (stressors) makes it stronger through adaptation. A child raised in an overly sterile environment develops a weak immune system—fragile to normal pathogens. Moderate, regular exposure creates resilience. Muscles are antifragile—they grow stronger when stressed by exercise (within limits; overtrain and they break down).

In business: A company that depends on one massive client is fragile. A company with 1,000 small clients is antifragile—losing one doesn't hurt much, and each client relationship teaches them how to serve customers better.

## Common Pitfalls
- Confusing antifragile with robust (they're different)
- Applying too much stress too quickly
- Forgetting that antifragility has limits
