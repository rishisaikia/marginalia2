## Definition

The tendency to rely too heavily on the first piece of information encountered (the "anchor") when making decisions, even if it's irrelevant or arbitrary.

## Key Insight

Your brain latches onto the first number or fact it encounters and uses it as a reference point for all subsequent judgments. This happens unconsciously, even when you know the anchor is random. Whoever sets the anchor controls the negotiation.

## How to Apply

Protect yourself from anchoring:
- Be aware of the first number mentioned in negotiations
- Generate your own anchor based on research before encountering others'
- Question whether initial information is relevant
- In negotiations, anchor first with an ambitious number

## Real-World Example

A shirt is marked $200, "on sale" for $100. You think "great deal!" But the shirt was never meant to sell for $200—that's an anchor making $100 seem reasonable. If you'd seen it priced at $100 first, you might think it's expensive.

In salary negotiations, the first number mentioned heavily influences the final outcome. If the employer says "The range is $60-80k," you'll likely settle near that range. If you say "I'm looking for $90-100k" first, the negotiation centers around that instead.

A real estate agent shows you three overpriced houses first, then shows you a merely expensive house. It feels reasonable by comparison—you've been anchored high. If they'd shown the expensive house first, you'd think it was too much.

Studies show that asking people "Is the population of Turkey more or less than 35 million?" before asking them to estimate Turkey's population yields much lower estimates than asking "More or less than 100 million?" first.

## Common Pitfalls

- Thinking you're immune to anchoring (you're not)
- Setting unrealistic anchors that kill negotiations
- Ignoring anchors entirely and appearing uninformed
