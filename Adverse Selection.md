## Definition
When buyers and sellers have different information, the people who accept an offer are systematically different from the people you'd want. The worst options are most likely to be selected.

## Key Insight
If you can't tell quality apart, you get the worst quality. People with bad options are eager to sell/accept; people with good options hold back. This creates a "death spiral" where only the worst remain. Understanding adverse selection explains why markets fail and how to design better systems.

## How to Apply
Recognize adverse selection:
- "Who's most eager to accept this deal?"
- "What do they know that I don't?"
- "Why would the good options agree to this?"

Combat it:
- Require credible signals of quality
- Screen carefully before selection
- Create incentives for good options to participate

## Real-World Example
**Used car market**: Sellers know if their car is good or a "lemon." Buyers can't tell. So buyers offer an average price. Owners of good cars won't sell for average price—only owners of lemons will. Buyers realize this and lower offers. More good cars exit. Eventually only lemons remain. This is the "Market for Lemons" problem.

**Health insurance**: People who know they're sick rush to buy insurance. Healthy people skip it. Insurance companies end up with sicker-than-average pools, must raise prices, which drives out more healthy people, making the pool sicker, requiring higher prices... death spiral from adverse selection.

**Job applications**: When you post "we'll accept anyone," you get applications from people who can't get jobs elsewhere. The best candidates are already employed or have multiple offers. Your open call adversely selects for lower quality.

**Online dating**: People who are easy to date in person spend less time on apps. People who struggle in person spend more time online. Apps adversely select for people with fewer other options (though this has normalized over time).

**Volunteer opportunities**: Ask "who can help?" and you get people with free time, not necessarily people with relevant skills. The most capable people are usually busy. You adversely select for availability over ability.

## Common Pitfalls
- Not recognizing when selection process favors worse options
- Assuming random selection when adverse selection is operating
- Creating systems that drive away high-quality participants
- Not requiring credible quality signals before selecting
